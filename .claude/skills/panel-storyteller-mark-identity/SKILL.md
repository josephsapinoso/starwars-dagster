# Mark identity is a spent currency, not free richness

A data mark carries exactly as much identity as the story has chosen to spend on that
item, at that beat. Uniformity is not a design gap to be filled — it is the census idea
made visible. Individuation (naming, highlighting, a face) is the story's finger
pointing; issue it to everyone and you point at nothing.

## Rules

1. **Name what the mark asserts before changing it.** A uniform dot asserts "one counted
   record, identity withheld." That withholding is load-bearing wherever the story trades
   on absence (the unweighed, the unknown-homeworld, the never-measured) — an archive of
   records is deliberately faceless; that is what makes a missing field read as a gap
   rather than a personality. A face asserts "a known individual." Only items the story
   ALREADY names on-stage may honestly make that assertion; inventing identity for the
   rest claims data the archive does not hold (a story-honesty twin of data-honesty).

2. **Individuation is spent per beat, deliberately.** The state grammar already spends it:
   base (counted, anonymous) / dim / faint (receded) / hot (the one to look at, named).
   The strongest individuation token — a face/portrait — belongs ONLY where the story
   already chose to individuate: the featured/hot mark at the beat it is named. Free
   individuation for all N marks is spent payoff: the emphasis state has nothing left to
   do when every mark already shouts.

3. **Recognizability front-runs payoffs.** If the payoff is "these specific items
   persisted / are special," and those items are the most ICONIC (the droids, the villain,
   the mascot), making all marks recognizable from beat 0 lets the reader's eye lock onto
   the payoff items the whole way down — the reveal is pre-spoiled by recognizability, not
   by any label a spoiler-pin can catch. Faces leak payoffs through the eye, silently.

4. **Turn the constraint into the reveal (the closest-surviving form).** When "identity on
   every mark" is rejected, the salvage that KEEPS the fun is: the mark resolves into a
   face at the moment it is named — the dot becomes a portrait as it goes hot. This
   preserves the census (base marks stay uniform), sharpens the pointing gesture (the face
   IS the emphasis), and STRENGTHENS the payoff: anonymous dots turning into faces at the
   reveal beat is a better reveal visual than dots-with-gold. Restricting faces to the
   named subset also dissolves the honesty problem — the named items are exactly the ones
   with canonical faces. "Fun" that lands on the payoff beat serves the arc; "fun"
   sprayed across every beat competes with it.

5. **Feasibility follows the narrative cut.** Faces-on-featured-marks-only is also the
   legibility answer (featured marks are scaled up and few, so the mobile "reads at ~3px"
   wall never applies) and the bloat answer (a handful of sprites, not N). Let the story
   ruling shrink the engineering problem before the engineers cost the maximal one; note
   the convergence but defer sizing/palette/feasibility to the graphic-designer/engineer.
